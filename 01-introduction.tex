\section{Introduction}\label{sec:introduction}

\IEEEPARstart{R}{einforcement} Learning (RL) has become the defining
mechanism of the Large Language Model (LLM) post-training pipeline.
After pre-training and Supervised
Fine-Tuning (SFT), it is RL transforms a fluent text generator into a goal-directed policy optimized for task-level rewards, providing the decision-making basis for an agentic system. Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback
(RLHF)~\cite{christiano2017deeprl} enabled the
first generation of instruction-following assistants; Direct Preference
Optimization (DPO)~\cite{rafailov2023dpo} demonstrated that the same
alignment objective could be achieved without an explicit reward model;
and Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO) with verifiable rewards
produced models whose reasoning capabilities are substantially shaped by
RL~\cite{shao2024deepseekmath}. Most recently, this technology has been extended beyond single responses to optimize multi-step behavior, in which an LLM plans, invokes tools, maintains memory, and acts in dynamic environments.~\cite{zhang2026landscape}.

This progress, however, has not been matched by a comparable effort to organize the field conceptually. Terminology
overlaps across paradigms, algorithms proliferate with variants such as
Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO), DPO, GRPO, and REINFORCE
Leave-One-Out (RLOO), and reward designs range from scalar human
preferences to dense step-level verifiers. Several surveys address parts
of this landscape: some focus on RLHF
alignment~\cite{kaufmann2024survey},
others catalogue broad agent
architectures~\cite{wang2024agentsurvey},
and a recent comprehensive taxonomy organizes agentic RL by capabilities
and task domains~\cite{zhang2026landscape}. Yet none provides a
compact, tutorial-oriented synthesis that places any given method
along a small set of clearly defined axes. Practitioners entering the
field still lack a concise map connecting preference-based fine-tuning to
the current frontier of agentic RL.

This survey fills that gap with a unified treatment of RL for LLM
training, from its inception in preference-based fine-tuning to
multi-step agentic optimization, organized around a three-axis
taxonomy. We formalize, within a single Markov Decision Process (MDP)
framework, the paradigm shift from single-step Preference-Based
Reinforcement Fine-Tuning (PBRFT) to agentic RL,
and we summarize the algorithmic spectrum that spans REINFORCE, PPO,
DPO, and GRPO. On this basis we propose a taxonomy that classifies
methods along three axes, the reward signal, the
optimization algorithm, and the training scope (single response or
multi-step trajectory), so that every method occupies a unique,
well-defined cell. We then review representative works along each axis,
covering reward design, optimization algorithms, and agentic
trajectories, and assemble the results into a table that assigns every method to its cell in the taxonomy.
Finally, we synthesize the open challenges of the field, among them
reward hacking, credit assignment, sample efficiency, scalable
oversight, and evaluation, and we point to concrete research
directions.

The remainder of this paper is organized as follows.
Section~\ref{sec:background} formalizes the RL framework for LLMs and
recalls core algorithms. Section~\ref{sec:taxonomy} introduces the
three-axis taxonomy. Sections~\ref{sec:reward}, \ref{sec:optimization},
and~\ref{sec:agentic} examine each axis in turn.
Section~\ref{sec:challenges} discusses open problems, and
Section~\ref{sec:conclusion} concludes.
